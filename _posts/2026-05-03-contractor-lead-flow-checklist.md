Most contractors do not need more leads before they know what is happening to the leads they already get.

The problem usually hides in ordinary places: a call that goes to voicemail while the owner is on site, a web form that lands in one inbox, an estimate that gets sent with no reminder attached, or a Google review request that depends on someone remembering at the end of a long day.

The Contractor Lead Flow Audit is a simple way to inspect those weak points before you book an assessment, buy software, or hire another agency. This post walks through some of the ideas behind it. The full checklist is on the site and is meant to be downloaded, printed, and marked up against your actual business.

## Start where the customer first reaches out

The first section of the checklist is about capture. If someone calls, fills out a form, messages your Google Business Profile, or replies to a Facebook post, where does that lead go?

This sounds basic, but it is where a lot of money disappears. A contractor can have a decent website and still lose jobs because the phone number is not clickable on mobile, the form notification goes to one person, or after-hours calls sit until the next morning.

The checklist asks you to look for signs like:

1. Whether missed calls trigger a text-back or internal alert.
2. Whether form submissions create a visible lead somewhere.
3. Whether after-hours inquiries get a clear next step.
4. Whether the business knows which source the lead came from.

You do not need a complicated system to fix all of that. Sometimes the best first fix is turning on something already included in Jobber, your phone system, your CRM, or your website form tool.

## Then inspect the handoff

Leads usually leak when responsibility changes hands.

Maybe the office admin takes the call, then the owner needs to quote it. Maybe the owner takes the call from a job site, then a helper needs to schedule it. Maybe a form comes in from Google Ads, then it needs to become an actual appointment.

The checklist pushes you to answer a blunt question: what is the next visible action after each lead arrives?

If the answer is "I remember to call them later," that is not a system. It might work on a slow week, but it breaks the moment the phone gets busy, a crew needs help, or the owner spends the day in the field.

## Estimate follow-up is usually the quietest leak

Contractors notice missed calls because they feel immediate. Estimate follow-up is harder to see because the job almost happened.

The homeowner got the quote. The work looked like a fit. Then nothing happened.

The checklist does not give away the full follow-up framework, but it does force you to check whether every sent estimate has:

1. An owner.
2. A next date.
3. A follow-up method.
4. A clear lost reason if the job does not close.

Without those four things, your pipeline is mostly a memory test.

## Reviews and referrals need a trigger

Most contractors ask for reviews randomly. Some ask only when they remember. Some ask only when a customer says something nice in person.

That is not enough.

Reviews should be attached to a job closeout event. Referral requests should be attached to the moment a customer is happiest with the work. The checklist includes prompts for checking whether the request is written, repeatable, and assigned to someone.

The goal is not to automate the human relationship out of the business. The goal is to make sure the ask actually happens.

## Source tracking keeps you from guessing

If you do not know which calls became estimates and which estimates became booked jobs, your marketing reports are mostly decoration.

A business can spend money on Google Ads, Facebook, SEO, door hangers, referrals, and lead platforms, then still have no idea which one produced profitable work. The checklist includes source tracking because it changes the quality of every later decision.

The useful question is not just "How many leads did this source create?"

The useful question is "Which source created jobs we actually wanted, at margins that made sense?"

## Use the checklist before you buy another tool

The checklist is intentionally practical. It is not a software comparison guide, and it is not a pitch for AI.

Run through it first. Mark the parts that are already handled. Circle the parts that are loose. If you find three or more gaps, the next move is usually not a big rebuild. It is a short list of fixes in the order that will recover the most time or revenue.
